I · Purpose

Why families hire a Private Mentorship Assistant.

Most support providers are contracted for specific hours and specific tasks. A tutor teaches academic material during booked sessions. A caregiver manages routines at home. A case manager handles paperwork from their office. Each provider does their contracted job and stops there.

The Private Mentorship Assistant covers the work that falls between those providers. This includes attending the psychological assessment feedback session, sitting in the CLBC planning meeting, completing funding applications that have been sitting unfinished for months, showing up for the first week of a vocational placement, speaking with an employer after an incident on the job, and travelling with the client to a neighbourhood they have not navigated alone before.

One Assistant handles this work for one family. The Assistant is hired by the family, accountable to the family, and carries context across every provider and every institution the client interacts with. The Assistant does not replace schools, caregivers, or case managers. The Assistant does the coordination work that sits between them.

II · Representation

The Assistant attends meetings with the client.

With signed consent from the family, the Assistant attends the institutional meetings the client needs to attend: classrooms, psychological vocational assessments, CLBC and WorkBC planning meetings, funding appointments, workplace reviews, and any other decision-making conversation the family would otherwise attend alone or send the client into alone.

The Assistant's job in the room is to listen, take organized notes, and keep track of what is decided. The client remains the primary voice in the conversation. The Assistant watches for questions the client wants to ask but may not raise in the moment, and makes sure nothing the family agreed on in advance is left out of the final plan.

When the situation requires it, the Assistant speaks on the client's behalf. If an accommodation was agreed to and is not being followed, the Assistant says so on the record. If a clinician or counsellor uses language the client does not understand, the Assistant translates it in the moment. If a planner recommends supports that do not match the plan the family and Assistant have already built, the Assistant restates the correct plan out loud.

The same Assistant attends every meeting. The Assistant present at a WorkBC intake is the same Assistant at the psychologist's feedback session, the funding meeting, and the first day of the vocational placement. Institutions see many families each week. The Assistant carries the context of this one family into every room.

III · Resourcing

The Assistant finds programs and files applications.

Most institutional programs have prerequisites before a client can access them: a referral from the correct source, a funding stream that matches the client's profile, a long-form application with specific medical documentation attached, and a coordinator who decides whether a file moves forward. The Assistant handles these prerequisites for the family.

The Assistant identifies which programs the client qualifies for — CLBC eligibility, WorkBC intake, vocational training cohorts, ESL programs, supported volunteer placements — and completes the applications the family has not had time to complete: the Disability Tax Credit, the Registered Disability Savings Plan, the Persons with Disabilities designation, and other federal and provincial funding requests. The Assistant also tracks the follow-up correspondence that decides whether a file is approved or is closed.

The Assistant secures funded supports the family would otherwise pay for out of pocket. For example: a psychological vocational assessment typically costs around $4,000 when paid privately. For one past client, the full cost of the assessment was paid for by WorkBC after the Assistant secured the correct referral. Our job is to know which funded pathways exist for which clients, and to route the family accordingly.

Over time, the Assistant builds working relationships with the people inside these institutions — which coordinator decides cohort placements, which counsellor filters applications, which clinician turns a report around in weeks rather than months. One family interacts with each institution too rarely to build that knowledge. The Assistant builds it across clients and uses it to shorten the time between a need and a resource.

IV · Reinforcement

The Assistant reports to the family and re-teaches the client.

Every meeting the Assistant attends generates information and decisions. Without a deliberate process for reporting that information to the family and re-teaching it to the client, most of what is said in an institutional meeting is forgotten within a day.

The Assistant's process runs in three steps. (1) During the meeting, the Assistant takes organized notes — a record of what was said, who said it, and what was decided. (2) After the meeting, the Assistant sends the family a written summary. The summary is delivered the same day where possible, and it explains what was agreed, what is still pending, and what the family needs to know before the next appointment. (3) The Assistant then books a separate session with the client to re-teach the material at the client's pace, away from the original setting — whether the material is forklift certification content, a psychologist's report, a WorkBC counsellor's plan, or a new transit route.

Most other support providers do not offer this re-teach. A tutor covers academic material during the tutoring block and leaves. A case manager makes a decision and moves on. A clinician explains a diagnosis once, in professional language, in a single appointment. The Private Mentorship Assistant returns to the material afterward with the client until the client can use it without help.

Over time, this process gives the family a continuous written record of every institutional interaction, and gives the client repeated exposure to the material instead of a single appointment the client may not remember on their own.

V · Where we operate

Where the Assistant works.

The list below is the set of institutions and settings where Private Mentorship Assistants attend with clients. Signed consent from the family authorizes the Assistant to attend meetings, receive documentation, and — when required — speak on the record. The list is not exhaustive. It covers the settings where the work most often takes place.

SchoolsK–12, post-secondary, vocational
Where the school permits, the Assistant attends classes with the client. The Assistant takes notes during instruction, identifies material the client is not retaining, and re-teaches it afterward. For clients in vocational programs, ESL courses, or structured post-secondary programs, the Assistant's presence is often the difference between completing the program and withdrawing.
CLBCCommunity Living British Columbia
The provincial agency that funds supports for adults with developmental disabilities. The Assistant attends facilitator meetings, planning sessions, and service reviews with the client, translates what is said into language the family understands, and makes sure the supports the client needs are written into the plan.
WorkBCProvincial employment services
British Columbia's publicly funded employment program. The Assistant attends intakes, employment counsellor meetings, and skills assessments with the client, and manages the correspondence that moves the client through the system.
Back in MotionVocational training & return-to-work
A contracted provider that runs vocational training and return-to-work programs across the Lower Mainland. Private Mentorship clients enter these programs through the Assistant, who attends intake, coursework, workplace placements, and the case-management meetings that set next steps.
Psychological Vocational AssessmentsFormal evaluations
A formal evaluation by a registered psychologist that identifies the client's cognitive profile, learning needs, and realistic employment pathways. The Assistant attends the referral, intake, and feedback session with the client, and translates the report into a plan the family can use. The Assistant also arranges funded pathways for the assessment where available (see Section III).
Federal & Provincial FundingDTC, RDSP, PWD, and related streams
This covers the Disability Tax Credit, the Registered Disability Savings Plan, the Persons with Disabilities designation, and related provincial assistance programs. The Assistant compiles the medical documentation, completes the long-form applications, and tracks the follow-up correspondence — work that usually sits unfinished for months in households without support.
WorkplacesPaid positions & supported placements
Whether the client is in a paid position, a supported placement, or a pre-employment trial, the Assistant attends on-site where the employer allows. The Assistant speaks with supervisors about accommodations, coaches the client through early shifts, and intervenes when a miscommunication would otherwise cost the client the placement.
Volunteer PlacementsCommunity & skills-building roles
Volunteer placements are often the first workplace environment the client enters. The Assistant is present for onboarding, early shifts, and the check-ins with the placement organization, so the experience builds toward paid employment.

VI · Eligibility & engagement

Who this service is for.

The Private Mentorship Assistant is not for every family. It is designed for families navigating a specific pattern of coordination work. If any of the following describes your household, the Assistant is likely the right fit.

  • A young adult family member is entering, attending, or graduating from a vocational, ESL, or structured learning program and needs a consistent adult in the room.
  • The family is working with CLBC, WorkBC, or preparing for a psychological vocational assessment, and does not want to attend those meetings alone.
  • A funding application — DTC, RDSP, PWD, or a similar program — has been sitting unfinished for months.
  • The client is in a supported workplace or volunteer placement, and a consistent on-site advocate would keep the placement working.
  • A parent is aging, working full-time, or caring for other children, and the weekly coordination work is no longer manageable.
  • The family has hired tutors, caregivers, or case managers separately and found that none of them, on their own, covered enough of the week.

Engagement begins with a conversation, not a form. Scope, access, and reporting cadence are set out in a signed agreement before the Assistant attends any institutional meeting on the client's behalf. The first step is described below.

How to begin.

We accept a limited number of families at a time. The first step is a conversation — no intake form, no paperwork. We will walk through the client's current week, identify the gaps, and decide together whether a Private Mentorship Assistant is the right fit. If it is, we move to a formal agreement.
