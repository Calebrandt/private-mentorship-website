Overview

Disability support at Private Mentorship focuses on independence, community access, and the daily routines that keep life moving forward. Each Private Family Assistant is matched to the client's specific support needs, interests, and goals. The plan is built at intake with the family and the client, and revisited as life changes.

We support adults and youth with autism, intellectual disabilities, physical mobility limitations, brain injury, mental-health needs, and chronic illness. Support is companion-level and life-skill focused — we are not clinical staff and we do not provide behavior intervention or nursing care.

What the Assistant helps with

  • Life-skill practice — budgeting, cooking, hygiene, transit, household routines
  • Community access — gym, library, classes, programs, community events
  • Communication and social practice in real settings
  • Transportation to appointments and programs
  • Job coaching — résumé, applications, interview prep, on-the-job support
  • Post-secondary support — coursework, organization, deadlines
  • Medication reminders at scheduled times (we do not administer)
  • ESL and academic tutoring when relevant

How support is built

At intake we discuss goals, daily structure, and the support areas most important to the client and family. The Assistant follows that plan and you revisit it monthly or as needs evolve. Support is hands-on and skills-based — focused on building independence in the areas the client and family decide matter most.

How matching works

Tell us at intake about the client's specific disability and the kind of support they want — autism, intellectual disability, physical mobility, mental health, brain injury. We match an Assistant on experience, personality fit, and the support areas the client wants to develop. Matching is the most important step; we don't rush it.

Where does support happen?

Wherever the client and family want it to. Most clients split time between home and community — gym, transit, programs, classes, friends' homes. Outings are part of the plan and the family covers external costs (transit fares, meals, tickets, the Assistant's portion when applicable).

What's outside our scope?

Behavior support requiring clinical certification, medication administration, nursing tasks, and crisis intervention. For clients with these needs we coordinate with the existing care team but do not replace them.

Common questions

Are Assistants trained for specific disabilities?

Matched on experience and fit, not formal clinical certification. We choose Assistants for emotional intelligence, patience, and lived experience supporting people with disabilities. For clients needing clinical-level care, we work alongside your existing team rather than replacing it.

Do you work with adults transitioning out of supported living?

Yes. Tier I or II covers the daily structure. Short-Term Live-In is sometimes used during the move itself. We coordinate with the client's previous staff during handover when permitted.

How does funding work?

Most clients private-pay. We also work with ICBC (post-accident rehab), CLBC (Community Living BC adult funding), and AFU (Autism Funding Unit, ages 6–18) when applicable. The intake call covers funding paperwork.

Can family members be the Assistant?

No. Independence from the family is part of the value of an outside Assistant. We support family members in their own role through coordination and check-ins.

Do you provide behavior support or behavior intervention?

No. Behavior support is a clinical specialty requiring different training and supervision. We coordinate with behavior consultants and clinical staff when they're part of the client's team, but we don't do behavior intervention ourselves.

What if the client and Assistant don't match well?

We rematch within the contract at no charge during the first month. Matching is the most-iterated part of disability work — we'd rather get it right by week three than push through.
